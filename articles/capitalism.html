Capitalism & Value

30/01/2026 · 9 min read

Feudalism

Let's start with Feudalism, which was a economic system that dominated Europe until the 1500s. In this system, wealth came from land, not money. And land came from inheritance. Nobles or lords owned land while peasants worked and exchanged labour for protection or a share of the goods produced. There was very little market exchange, most goods produced were for direct consumption and not for sale. Economic activity was for survival, not for accumulation or profit. Money existed mainly for luxury trade between the elites (like for silk, spices or fine metals). Value came from use, and wealth was tangible instead of abstract.

Mercantilism

Then 1600-1700s was Mercantilism. The nation-state tried to accumulate wealth by running trade surpluses, where exports were encouraged and imports were discouraged with tariffs. Govts supported merchant ventures. Colonies supplied raw materials cheaply, while mother countries produced finished goods. Money moved more actively for trade, but still under state control. This was the birth of the merchant class, people who realised that money could generate more money through trade. It represented a shift away from "use-value" and toward "exchange-value".

Early and industrial capitalism

1700-1800s was early capitalism, merchants began reinvesting profits to produce their own goods (factories). Labour became a commodity, workers exchanged labour for wages. Profit motivated mass production began and the end goal was no longer trade, but continuous growth. Money is now being used to generate more money.

19th century onwards is industrial capitalism. Money began to make money at a massive scale, profit & growth became the driving goals of the entire economy.

Use-value vs exchange-value

In older economies, things were valued for what they did. Based on their effectiveness, they held value. For example a home's value was as a shelter. But then with capitalism, they gained exchange value, which is the value they can be sold for. A home is now valued at its market price. And it follows that anything, even beauty, friendship and especially time can be commodified. As a result, money because the universal language. This simplifies exchange, making it easier to value things and compare, but also flattens meaning. Love and labour get expressed in the same unit, even though they should never be compared. Additionally, workers in feudalism at least saw the fruit of their own harvest. In capitalism, a worker toils away just to generate profit for their employer, and they become alienated and separated from the final product.

Capitalist realism

Capitalism became so pervasive and deeply rooted in their society that there came about a name for this phenomenon, capitalist realism. Capitalist realism is the idea that capitalism is the singular valid and correct economic system, and that every other economic system is worse. People admit that capitalism has its downfalls, but the common rebuttal is that it is "not as bad as other systems". Around the time when the berlin wall fell and communism died with the dissolution of the soviet union in 1991, was when history ended. Society as a whole accepted that capitalism was the only way, and that there was no other viable alternative. We accepted capitalism as the gospel truth, because all other systems had "failed".

But what can be done about this? The influential thinker Max Weber suggests that capitalism cannot be abolished easily. Instead, he wanted individuals to resist becoming purely economic beings. He points to a singular human desire to regain meaning in a meaningless world, by finding joy in things that cant be priced.

Minimum wage as resistance

One example is minimum wage laws. These laws aim to address one of the main pitfalls of capitalism- the redefinition of labour as just a commodity. Employers pay as little as possible to increase profit margins while workers can only accept what they are given. If left unrestricted, wages could fall below what is needed to live. By introducing a minimum wage, the government is declaring that if you give your time to work, you deserve enough to live decently.

It is an attempt to balance economic efficiency with human dignity. Declaring that labour has a minimum "dignity" or a minimum value of one's time regardless of how it is used puts a boundary on how far market logic can devalue human life. In a sense, it is a mini form of socialism within capitalism, by conveying the idea that a worker's life is not a commodity. It has meaning outside its exchange value, and life is worth more than hours worked.
