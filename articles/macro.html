Trump, the Fed, and the Coming World Order

05/02/2026 · 11 min read

Fed independence is one of those concepts that sounds bureaucratic until you understand why it exists. Politicians think in election cycles — usually four years. A central bank that answers to politicians will inevitably make decisions that feel good now but damage the economy later. The Fed's independence is a structural defence against that conflict of interest.

Trump's attacks on the Fed aren't just noise. They're part of a deliberate strategy to control the US dollar via interest rates — and to use a weaker dollar to revive American manufacturing. To understand why, you have to understand what interest rates actually do.

Interest rates and the dollar

The interest rate the Fed sets isn't the rate you get on a car loan. It's the baseline cost of money across the entire US economy. When it moves, everything moves with it: mortgages, business investment, credit card debt, stock prices — and most importantly, the US dollar itself.

A higher rate makes US assets more attractive to foreign investors (better return on investment), which drives up demand for dollars and strengthens the currency. A lower rate does the opposite. This mechanism — borrowing in a low-rate currency to invest in a high-rate one — is called a carry trade. The yen carry trade is one of the most watched examples.

Why Trump wants a weaker dollar

There are two Americas. Financial America benefits from a strong dollar: cheap imports, rising asset prices, large financial markets, debt-driven growth. Its main exports are dollars, Treasury bonds, and financial products. Then there's Manufacturing America, which benefits from a weaker dollar — it makes imported inputs (labour, raw materials) cheaper and domestic goods more competitive abroad.

Trump has been explicit: he wants to bring manufacturing back. He tried tariffs first. Now he's targeting the Fed. A rate cut weakens the dollar, making US exports more competitive and imported goods more expensive — effectively the same economic pressure as a tariff, but through monetary policy rather than trade policy.

The deeper reason this matters: after WW2, the US offered military protection to allies in exchange for using the dollar as the global reserve currency. That arrangement let the US economy grow even as manufacturing was outsourced — everyone needed dollars regardless. But if USD reserve currency status erodes, outsourced manufacturing becomes a strategic liability, not just an economic inefficiency.

The BRICS challenge and the gold corridor

Trump's tariffs became a catalyst that unified a coalition that had been loosely forming for years. BRICS — Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa — now has a concrete reason to accelerate de-dollarisation. The concern isn't just political; it's structural. In 2022, the US froze $600 billion of Russia's foreign reserves. That moment fundamentally damaged trust in the dollar as a neutral reserve asset. Countries watched and quietly began reducing their dollar holdings.

The more significant development is China's Shanghai Gold Exchange (SGE) and what I'm calling the gold corridor. Since Nixon ended the gold standard in 1971, currency has been fiat — backed by government trust rather than physical reserves. This freed governments to create credit and expand money supply, which is arguably the defining mechanism of modern capitalism. But it also means the dollar's value ultimately rests on confidence in American institutions.

China is attempting something that hasn't been tried since 1971: backing trade settlement with physical gold. The problem previously was that the gold was stored inside China — meaning you'd have to trust China instead of the US, which wasn't an improvement for most countries. The gold corridor solves this by placing vaults across BRICS nations, decentralising custody. Emerging markets now have a credible alternative to the dollar that doesn't require trusting either superpower unconditionally.

Where this leaves us

I wrote the original version of this in November 2025, when I started forming the view that a new world order was coming. Now, with Trump's second term behaviour in 2026, that view looks less like a prediction and more like an observation. The US is voluntarily stepping back from its role as global insurer. Trump is, perhaps unintentionally, handing China the credibility it needs to position the yuan as a legitimate reserve alternative.

None of this means the dollar collapses next year. Reserve currency transitions take decades. But the direction has shifted. The question for investors isn't whether this happens — it's how fast, and which assets benefit as the transition unfolds.
