the 20th century's topic of contention was capitalism vs. the state; the 21st century's is the merger of the two

13/07/2026 · 9 min read

The Nvidia market cap at around $3.3T places it next to Germany and Japan in world GDP rankings. MC (market cap) represents the market's opinion on the value of a company, i.e. anticipated future extraction of value. So the fact that Nvidia is so high up there means the market is pricing in HUGE value, primarily from infrastructural indispensability. NVDA is now functioning like a nation-state. By having such a chokehold on AI, it unilaterally controls the means of production on a civilizational level. A monopoly of this size with such deep integration into any AI workflow means that if Jensen Huang were to wake up one day and decide to stop making GPUs, the world would genuinely go dark. We are seeing more and more firms begin acquiring monopolies on legitimate INFRASTRUCTURE which is increasingly important and consequential in a digital economy.

An analysis on the relationship between firms and nations

Jurisdictional authority: constant legal battles between firms & nations e.g. Apple v FBI (encryption), Meta v EU (data sovereignty). Nations also fighting back against firms e.g. US CHIPS act.

Symbiosis:

Reasons states need firms: privatisation to maximise efficiency, advancing technological capability etc. End stage is when the firm BECOMES the state apparatus e.g. Elon's DOGE.

Reasons firms need states: IP protection, physical infrastructure. End stage is when the state BECOMES the firm's apparatus e.g. US trade policy used as Nvidia export control to establish US AI dominance.

Do firms need states more, or do states need firms more? Factors that influence the degree of dependency: capital mobility of the firm, control of (overly) advanced infrastructure that the state doesn't understand, sector: energy/defense firms depend more on states while tech has flipped the script.

So the question now is, what differentiates a firm from a nation?

Typically, firms exist by permission of states. They are subject to national law, while a nation does not submit to any higher authority. But this distinction is eroding. What do you do when a firm is too big to sanction? What kind of punishment could be imposed on Nvidia that would actually have an effect? Firms like these acquire a de facto sovereignty through indispensability.

What about nationality? You are born into citizenship of a country, and you don't have a choice in that. Firm membership or patronisation is technically optional, but when Google controls search, Amazon controls shopping, and Nvidia controls AI, there is no functional alternative. There is an illusion of choice but the magnitude of monopoly erodes its value. There is just no better alternative hence you are forced to follow the masses and contribute even more to large monopolies.

Nations are typically incentivized by societal welfare, and held accountable by democratic mechanisms, while firms are held accountable to shareholders. One is welfare-based, and one is return-based. Even here, the line is blurred. Governments are starting to be run like businesses, with the core idea that the profit incentive is the ultimate cure. By targeting returns and maximising efficiency in a government, it is assumed that a cheaper government is better. By privatising everything and handing control over to private firms, costs can be minimised. And that is *usually* good. The perfect example is in the US where ROI is prioritised over societal welfare. Trump would say "stocks are up" MUCH more frequently ..than "grocery prices are down". Tariffs are being framed and justified as revenue sources rather than geopolitical tools, while the NATO alliance is seen as a financial burden. Governmental "efficiency" and cost-cutting is so blatantly profit maximising. The word efficiency should never come up in a conversation about societal welfare, because they are mutually exclusive. You cannot have social welfare and be efficient at the same time.

Okay, so clearly there are many overlaps between the nation and the firm. This is concerning, but is it normal? Should it be normal? What is wrong with a firm being on the same level as a nation?

The firm's purpose is pure capital accumulation for its owners. A nation cannot focus on capital accumulation; its aim is the flourishing of ALL members, including the unproductive ones. Children, the elderly, the disabled, and the unemployed are all stakeholders in a nation but would never be in a firm. When a nation adopts profit maximising logic, it reclassifies them from citizens to liabilities. And what ensues is clearly visible in everyday life. Inequality, especially wealth inequality, rampant amongst almost all major economies on this earth. A government that behaves like a firm will inevitably deprioritize anyone who doesn't generate returns. By design, firms redistribute wealth upwards, where returns go back to capital. Nations *should* redistribute downward through taxation, public goods, and social insurance. These are the building blocks to form the social conditions that a market requires to operate. But now we see things like unfair taxation policies and reductions in the provision of public goods. Instead, wealth is redistributed upwards just like a firm. Finally, and perhaps most importantly, we need to talk about the differing timeframes of firms and nations. Nations have an interGENERATIONAL (think about that word) obligation to protect or ration out resources across many decades, perhaps even a century. Firms will operate on short term value extraction logic with a timeframe of a single decade at best. So when firms like Nvidia acquire nation-like power, resources like the atmosphere, or the climate get subjected to short term value extraction in a way that is unsustainable across generations.

The welfare state, public goods and general democratic rights were never defining features of capitalism; they were won through labour movements and revolutions. They were political impositions on capitalism. However, late-stage unrestricted technocapitalism represents a reversal of these impositions through the gradual replacement of political logic with market logic, until the distinction between citizen and consumer is nullified.
